Bihar ( Hindi: Bihāra , pronounced [bɪˈɦaːr] , ⓘ) is a state in Eastern India. It is the second largest state by population, the 12th largest by area, and the 15th largest by GDP in 2021.[11][12][13] Bihar borders Uttar Pradesh to its west, Nepal to the north, the northern part of West Bengal to the east, and Jharkhand to the south. Bihar is split by the river Ganges, which flows from west to east.[5] On 15 November 2000, southern Bihar was ceded to form the new state of Jharkhand.[14] Only 11.27% of the population of Bihar lives in urban areas as per a 2020 report.[15] Additionally, almost 58% of Biharis are below the age of 25, giving Bihar the highest proportion of young people of any Indian state.[16] The official language is Hindi, which shares official status alongside that of Urdu.
The main native languages are Maithili, Magahi and Bhojpuri. But there are several other languages being spoken at smaller levels.

history

Ancient Bihar: Magadha's Rise: Bihar, particularly the region of Magadha (modern-day Patna), was the center of several powerful empires, including the Nanda, Mauryan, and Gupta empires. Founding of Empires: The Mauryan Empire, founded by Chandragupta Maurya, and the Gupta Empire, known for its golden age, had their capitals in Pataliputra (modern-day Patna). Birthplace of Religions: Buddhism and Jainism, two of India's major religions, originated in Bihar. Notable Rulers: Chandragupta Maurya, Bindusara, Ashoka, Sri Gupta, and Samudragupta were some notable rulers of ancient Bihar. Ancient Sites: Bihar boasts a rich heritage of ancient sites, including Bodh Gaya, where Buddha attained enlightenment, and Nalanda, a renowned ancient university. Medieval Bihar: Sultanate Era: After the fall of the Gupta Empire, Bihar came under the rule of various dynasties, including the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal Empire. Dynasties: The Oiniwar dynasty ruled North Bihar as vassals of the Delhi Sultanate. Mughal Influence: After the fall of the Suri dynasty in 1556, Bihar came under the Mughal Empire. Staging Post for British: Later, Bihar became the staging post for the British colonial Bengal Presidency. British Rule and Beyond: Separate Province: On March 22, 1912, Bihar was carved out as a separate province in the British Indian Empire. Post-Independence: After India gained independence, Bihar became a state in the Indian Union. Modern Bihar: Bihar is known for its pilgrimage sites, vibrant culture, and vast history.

culture

Festivals: Chhath: This unique festival, celebrated primarily in Bihar, is dedicated to the worship of the Sun God (Surya). It involves ritual bathing, fasting, and prayers, often performed on the banks of a river or large water body. Other Festivals: Bihar also celebrates various Hindu festivals like Durga Puja, Holi, and Saraswati Puja. Arts and Crafts: Madhubani Paintings: These vibrant folk paintings, also known as Mithila paintings, are a hallmark of Bihar's art scene. Other Crafts: Bihar is also known for its miniature paintings, wall decorations, patchwork, and local handicrafts. Food: Litti Chokha: This is a very popular dish in Bihar, consisting of roasted wheat balls (litthi) served with mashed vegetables (chokha). Other Dishes: Other traditional dishes include Chana Ghugni and various regional sweets. Historical Significance: Nalanda University: The ruins of the ancient Nalanda University, a renowned center of learning, are located in Bihar. Bodh Gaya: Bodh Gaya, where Lord Buddha attained enlightenment, is also located in Bihar. Birthplace of Guru Gobind Singh: The 6th Guru of Sikhs, Guru Gobind Singh, was born in Bihar. Magadh Empire: Bihar was the heartland of the Magadh Empire, which included the Nanda, Maurya, and Gupta Empires. Pataliputra: The capital of the Magadh Empire, Pataliputra, is now known as Patna. Traditional Attire: Pancha: A traditional garment for men, typically consisting of a dhoti (a long piece of cloth wrapped around the waist and legs) paired with a kurta (a loose-fitting shirt). Saree: The traditional attire for women is the saree. Folk Dances: Jhijhiya: A famous folk dance of Bihar, performed by women in the mythological town of Mithila.

Tourism in Bihar

The state of Bihar (Hindi: बिहार) in eastern India, is one of the oldest inhabited places in the world with a history going back 3000 years. The rich culture and heritage of Bihar is evident from the innumerable ancient monuments that are dotted all over the state. Bihar is home to many tourist attractions and is visited by large numbers of tourists from all over the world.[1] Around total 6 million tourists visit Bihar every year.[1] Mahabodhi Temple, Bodh Gaya Ruins of Nalanda Mahavihara Rohtasgarh Fort at Rohtas Darbhanga Fort in Darbhanga Sher Shah Suri Tomb at Sasaram Mundeshwari Temple in Kaimur Deo Sun Temple in Aurangabad Janaki Kund at Sitamarhi Megasthenes (B.C. 350–290 B.C.) visited the region in reign of Chandragupta Maurya.[2] His observations were recorded in Indika. Dionysius was son of Megasthenes, who visited Pataliputra in reign of Ashoka. Hsuan-Tsang and I Ching visited Nalanda to study in the 7th century. This state is the place of Mahavira, the 24th and last Tirthankara, Aryabhata, Great Ashoka, Chanakya, Gautama Buddha, Mahavira, Guru Gobind Singh, Chandragupta Maurya, Vātsyāyana, Sher Shah Suri, Maa Tara Chandi Temple, and many other great historical figures.[3] Bihar ranked 8th in the top 10 states/UTs of India in number of foreign tourist visits in 2017.[4] The year 2019, saw highest number of tourists in Bihar, with over 35 million tourists, including 11.9 million foreign tourists.[5]
